CFD Analysis (ME 211)

CFD Analysis of Internal Water Flow Through a W-Shaped Channel

Computational fluid dynamics study of incompressible internal flow using ANSYS Fluent with laminar and turbulent models.

DateDecember 2025
CourseME 211 – Fluid Mechanics
TeamMir Yamin
InstitutionUniversity of Illinois at Chicago
ANSYS Fluent SolidWorks SpaceClaim MATLAB CFD Meshing

Introduction

This project investigates internal fluid flow through a 1-meter rectangular channel featuring a 753 mm W-shaped bottom geometry. A CAD model was created in SolidWorks, exported, and imported into ANSYS Workbench for meshing and CFD simulation. Three distinct analyses were performed under different inlet conditions and turbulence models to study how flow behavior, pressure distribution, and velocity profiles are affected by the channel geometry.

Channel Design & Geometry

The channel has a rectangular cross-section with the following dimensions:

Channel Height7 mm
Channel Width50 mm
Total Length1013 mm
W-Section Start120 mm from inlet
W-Section Length753 mm

The SolidWorks assembly was exported and imported into ANSYS SpaceClaim, where tolerances were resolved, parts were combined, and the internal fluid volume was extracted for physics simulation.

Mesh Generation

A structured mesh was generated with an inflation layer applied to all wall faces (excluding inlet and outlet) to resolve boundary layer effects:

  • Inflation Layers: 5 layers, smooth transition, ratio 0.272, growth rate 1.2
  • Element Size: 153 mm (optimized for ANSYS Student limit of ~1M elements)
  • Total Nodes: 396,728
  • Total Faces: 2,214,817
  • Total Elements: ~919,590

Named selections were created for inlet (inlet-x) and outlet (outlet-x) boundaries.

Simulation Cases

Case 1: Mass Flow – Laminar

By: Mir Yamin

  • Inlet: Mass flow rate = 0.0178 kg/s
  • Model: Laminar
  • Solver: 3D, double-precision, pressure-based, steady
  • Fluid: Water (998.2 kg/m³)
  • Wall: Aluminum, no-slip, 0 W/m² heat flux
  • Scheme: Coupled, 2nd-order upwind

Case 2: Mass Flow – Turbulent SST

By: Mir Yamin

  • Inlet: Mass flow rate = 0.0178 kg/s
  • Model: k–ω SST
  • Viscous heating enabled
  • Converged in ~88 iterations
  • Outlet velocity: 0.0509 m/s (matches theory)
  • Re ≈ 625 (laminar regime)

Case 3: Velocity Inlet 2 m/s

By: Mir Yamin

  • Inlet: Velocity = 2 m/s
  • Model: Laminar
  • Inlet temperature: 26.85°C
  • Converged in ~133 iterations
  • Outlet pressure: ~2431 Pa
  • Re » 625 (transitional regime)

Results & Discussion

Velocity Distribution

Cases 1 and 2 produced stable, fully developed parabolic velocity profiles with outlet velocity of 0.0509 m/s — exactly matching the theoretical prediction from V = ṁ/(ρA). No recirculation zones formed in the W-section at these low Reynolds numbers. Case 3 showed much steeper velocity gradients with acceleration bands along the W-profile’s sloped surfaces.

Pressure Distribution

Pressure decreased monotonically from inlet to outlet across all cases. Low-flow cases showed minimal pressure drops (1–2 Pa), while the 2 m/s case produced a pressure drop of ~2431 Pa — over two orders of magnitude greater, demonstrating the nonlinear dependence of frictional losses on inlet velocity.

Effect of W-Shaped Geometry

At low velocities, the W-section had minimal influence on flow behavior. At higher velocities (Case 3), the geometry produced zones of accelerated flow, increased shear stress, and sharper pressure gradients. This demonstrates that geometric surface features become significant as flow velocity increases.

Conclusions

  1. Low-velocity cases (laminar and turbulent models) produced nearly identical results, confirming the flow is physically laminar at Re ≈ 625.
  2. Theoretical velocity matched Fluent results exactly in both mass-flow cases, validating boundary conditions and solver accuracy.
  3. The 2 m/s velocity-inlet case revealed strong pressure losses, demonstrating expected frictional scaling with velocity.
  4. The W-shaped geometry influences flow only at higher speeds, where sharper gradients and increased wall shear become significant.
  5. All simulations converged successfully within the ANSYS Student mesh limits (~920k elements), confirming appropriate mesh quality and solver settings.
