Senior Design (ME-396)

Pentagon Robot – Five-Bar Parallel Planar Manipulator

An automated pick-and-place system using a five-bar parallel planar linkage mechanism, designed to bridge the gap between theoretical classroom concepts and real-world mechatronic applications. The robot performs high-speed object transfer with ±0.5 mm accuracy and ≤5-second cycle time.

Course ME-396 Senior Design I
Role Design & Simulation Lead
Team MIE-25 (5 Members)
Status Design Phase Complete

Abstract

The automation of material handling is a cornerstone of modern manufacturing efficiency, yet there remains a significant need for accessible, educational platforms that demonstrate these complex kinematic principles. The Pentagon Robot addresses this gap by designing a five-bar parallel planar manipulator capable of performing automated pick-and-place operations on spherical objects between two predefined matrices.

The methodology employs a rigorous Model-Based Design (MBD) approach. The mechanical architecture utilizes a closed-loop five-bar linkage driven by two base-mounted NEMA 17 stepper motors, a configuration selected through a formal decision matrix analysis. Kinematic control is achieved via an inverse kinematics model solved through the Law of Cosines, enabling precise end-effector positioning within a computed workspace.

Design Criteria & Requirements

⚙️ Functional Requirements

  • Five-bar parallel planar linkage mechanism
  • Closed-loop chain to minimize moving inertia
  • Higher acceleration vs. serial manipulators
  • Vacuum suction end-effector with pressure feedback

🎯 Performance Metrics

  • Full transfer cycle ≤ 5 seconds
  • Placement accuracy ± 0.5 mm
  • Pick → Move → Place → Return
  • Secure ball seating in matrix

💰 Design Constraints

  • Prototype budget: $350 maximum
  • Cost-effective 3D printing & off-the-shelf parts
  • Compact workspace footprint
  • Educational demonstration capability

📜 Engineering Standards

  • ASME Y14.5 – GD&T
  • ASTM B221 – Aluminum specs
  • IPC/WHMA-A-620 – Wire harness
  • IEC 60034 – Rotating machines

Kinematic Analysis & Workspace

The Pentagon Robot utilizes a five-bar parallel planar linkage mechanism. To achieve precise control over the end-effector position, the system relies on an Inverse Kinematics (IK) model derived from the Law of Cosines. For each motor base, the distance to the target point is calculated, and the joint angle is determined by combining the vector angle with an internal angle to select the “elbow-out” configuration, avoiding singularities.

The reachable workspace is defined as the intersection of the annular regions accessible by the two independent arms. Since the selected geometry is symmetric (L1 = L2), the maximum reach per arm is 2L1 = 500 mm. The code generates a filled contour map of the workspace, providing visual verification of reachability across the target area.

Kinematic Parameters

Parameter Value Description
Proximal Link (L1) 250 mm Arm attached to the motor
Distal Link (L2) 250 mm Forearm connecting to end-effector
Base Separation (d) 200 mm Distance between motor shafts
Workspace Radius (Rmax) 500 mm Maximum theoretical extension

End-Effector & Suction System

For picking and placing ping-pong balls, the team evaluated mechanical grippers and suction-based grabbers. After comparing advantages and limitations, a suction-based system was selected for its gentler handling, simpler control, and reliable grip on smooth spherical surfaces.

During operation, a pressure sensor monitors negative pressure levels and confirms when the ball has been securely lifted. This sensing logic is integrated directly into the inverse kinematics control loop — the robot does not advance to the next placement position until the sensor verifies a successful grip.

The suction assembly consists of a vacuum pump, flexible tubing, a suction cup, a T-junction, and the pressure sensor positioned at the pneumatic line where pressure changes are most representative of the suction cup’s contact state.

Design Iterations & Decision Matrix

Three primary design iterations were developed and evaluated to optimize the mechanical configuration of the linkages and actuation transmission:

Design A: Co-Planar

All linkages and joints on a single horizontal plane. Simplifies CAD but suffers from significant mechanical interference between crossed links.

Design B: Belt-Driven

Motors mounted remotely using timing belts to drive primary arms. High precision but introduces compliance, backlash, and added complexity.

Design C: Stacked Direct-Drive ✓

Base-mounted motors with linkage pairs separated by vertical offsets (spacers). Eliminates interference and maximizes workspace reach. Selected as final design.

Designs were evaluated based on Workspace Reach (Critical), Manufacturing Cost (Critical), and Assembly Simplicity. Design C scored highest across all weighted criteria.

Cost Analysis

🔧 Prototype (Phase 1)

Total fabrication cost: $229.00 — well below the $350 budget constraint. Leverages 3D printing (PLA) for structural links and off-the-shelf standardized components.

🏭 Mass Production (Phase 2)

Scaled analysis for 1,000 units enables 40–60% cost reduction through bulk purchasing, injection-molded ABS (replacing 3D printed PLA), and custom PCB integration replacing Arduino UNO + CNC Shield.

Preliminary Results

⏱ Cycle Time

Motion simulations confirm ≤ 5-second cycle for a complete Pick → Move → Place → Return operation.

🎯 Placement Accuracy

IK model achieves placement accuracy within ± 0.5 mm, verified through workspace contour analysis.

💰 Budget Compliance

Prototype cost of $229.00 is 34.6% under the $350 budget ceiling, leaving margin for contingencies.

🛠 Design Validation

Stacked Direct-Drive configuration eliminates all mechanical interference while maximizing workspace volume.

Conclusion & Next Steps

The Pentagon Robot project successfully completed the “Design and Simulation” phase. Through rigorous design iteration, the team selected a Stacked Direct-Drive configuration that eliminates mechanical interference and maximizes workspace reach while adhering to a simplified fabrication pipeline using 3D-printed PLA linkages and off-the-shelf components.

The next phase (Senior Design II) focuses on physical fabrication, assembly, and validation. This includes uploading firmware to the Arduino, calibrating the IK model against physical joint positions, and performing continuous-cycle testing to verify the ≤5-second cycle time and ±0.5 mm accuracy targets under real-world conditions.
